Gear Guide // REQUIRED · GOOD · BETTER · BEST
Before you buy anything: The required list is the absolute minimum to start. Everything else is an upgrade path — buy when your skills demand it, not before. Gear doesn't make you better. Reps make you better. Gear just stops being the limiting factor at a certain point. Items marked ✓ YOU OWN THIS are already in your setup.
Required Gear

You cannot start the program without these. Most you likely already have. None of them are expensive.

💻
Computer — Mac or PC
Any modern laptop or desktop made after 2015
Ableton Live runs on virtually any modern machine. 8GB RAM minimum, 16GB recommended. SSD storage makes a meaningful difference.
Already owned
🎛️
Ableton Live
Ableton Live Standard or Suite
The entire DAW curriculum is built around Ableton. Intro is too limited. Standard is sufficient. Suite adds instruments and effects worth having eventually. Free 90-day trial available.
Standard ~$449 · Suite ~$749 · Free trial at ableton.com
🔌
Audio Interface
Focusrite Scarlett Solo or 2i2 (2nd gen or newer)
Your computer's built-in audio is not suitable for recording or serious mixing. An interface gives you proper preamps, low-latency monitoring, and clean converters. The Scarlett line is the industry standard entry point.
Solo ~$120 · 2i2 ~$170
🎧
Closed-Back Headphones
Sony MDR-7506 or Audio-Technica ATH-M35
For tracking, mixing reference, and mobile work. You need isolation and a reasonably neutral frequency response. Both of these are industry standards.
MDR-7506 ~$100 · ATH-M35 ~$70
✓ YOU OWN BOTH
🎙️
Microphone — at least one
Shure SM57 or SM58 to start
The SM57 is the most versatile mic ever made — guitars, snare, vocals, amps. The SM58 is the go-to vocal mic. Either one starts you recording real instruments immediately. Both are indestructible.
SM57 ~$100 · SM58 ~$100
🎤
XLR Cable(s)
Mogami or Amazon Basics — at least 2
You need at least one to connect a mic to your interface. Buy two so you always have a backup. Cable problems cause more session failures than gear problems.
~$10-20 each
🎹
MIDI Controller / Keyboard
Arturia MiniLab or Akai MPK Mini
Essential for MIDI production in Ableton, programming drums, playing virtual instruments, and music theory exercises. You don't need 88 keys to start — 25 keys is enough.
~$100-130
🎧
Consumer Earbuds or Phone Speaker
Whatever you already own — AirPods, Samsung earbuds, phone speaker
Critical for cross-referencing your mixes. Every mix must be checked on a consumer playback system before it's called finished. This is not optional — it's part of the mixing workflow.
Already owned
✓ YOU OWN THIS
Optional Upgrades

Buy these when your skills demand it — when the gear is the limiting factor, not your knowledge. Each category shows Good / Better / Best with honest reasoning for each tier.

🎧
Mixing Headphones
You already own Good & Better
Good
✓ YOU OWN THIS
Audio-Technica ATH-M50x
~$150
The M50x is the upgraded version of your M35 — wider soundstage, better low-end extension, more detailed highs. Excellent for tracking and a solid mixing reference. Your M35 is very close to this tier already.
Better
✓ YOU OWN THIS
Sony MDR-7506
~$100
The industry standard closed-back reference headphone. Bright, detailed, unforgiving — exactly what you want for catching mix problems. The slightly hyped 8-10kHz means nothing hides. Already in your kit and already at this tier.
Best
Focal Listen Professional
~$500
Focal's closed-back mixing headphone is widely considered the best in its class. Exceptionally flat frequency response, incredible imaging, low fatigue over long sessions. The step-up from MDR-7506 that professional engineers reach for when mixing entirely on headphones. Buy this when your MDR-7506 feels like the ceiling.
🔊
Studio Monitors
Only useful in a treated space — skip until your room is right
Good
Yamaha HS5
~$400/pair
The "white woofer" Yamahas are brutally honest, lean in the low end, and unforgiving. If your mix sounds good on HS5s it'll translate almost everywhere. The professional starting point for monitors.
Better
Adam Audio T7V or Yamaha HS7
~$400-500 each
The Adam T7V adds ribbon tweeter detail that reveals high-frequency issues clearly. The HS7 gives you more low-end extension than the HS5. Both are step-ups that reward mixing skill you've already developed.
Best
Focal Alpha 65 Evo or Genelec 8040
~$900-1200 each
Focal's Alpha Evo series and Genelec's 8000 series are the monitors you find in professional mixing studios worldwide. Exceptionally flat, detailed, and reliable for translation. Don't buy these until you have acoustic treatment — an untreated room wastes them completely.
🎧
Open-Back Headphones
For mixing only — not tracking, not mobile
Good
AKG K240 Studio
~$70
Semi-open design, wide soundstage, very neutral midrange. The budget entry point into open-back mixing. Great for understanding stereo field and space in a mix.
Better
Beyerdynamic DT 990 Pro
~$180
Wide, airy soundstage. Slightly bright character in the highs — catch the same problems as your MDR-7506 but with better imaging. A favorite for mix engineers who work primarily on headphones.
Best
Focal Clear MG Professional
~$1,500
The reference standard for open-back mixing headphones. Extraordinary imaging, neutral frequency response, low fatigue. What the Focal Listen Professional is to closed-backs, the Clear MG is to open-backs. A long-term investment for a serious engineer.
🎙️
Condenser Microphone
For vocals, acoustic instruments, room sound
Good
Audio-Technica AT2020
~$100
The benchmark budget large-diaphragm condenser. Clean, neutral, works on everything. Used on countless professional recordings where the engineer knew how to use it. The starting point for condenser recording.
Better
Rode NT1 or Audio-Technica AT4040
~$270-350
The Rode NT1 is famous for its extremely low self-noise — almost silent, which matters in a mobile environment. The AT4040 has a flatter response and handles louder sources better. Both are genuinely professional tools.
Best
Neumann TLM 103 or U87
TLM 103 ~$1,200 · U87 ~$3,200
The TLM 103 is the most widely used professional vocal mic in the world. The U87 is the industry standard that's appeared on more hit records than any other microphone. Both reward a developed ear and good recording technique. Don't buy these until your technique justifies them.
🔌
Audio Interface
Upgrade when you need more inputs or better preamps
Good
Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 (3rd/4th gen)
~$170
Two inputs, clean preamps, reliable drivers, bus-powered. Everything you need for solo recording and mixing. The starting point for the entire industry.
Better
Universal Audio Volt 276 or Focusrite Scarlett 4i4
~$300-350
The UA Volt 276 includes a 1176-style compressor on the input channel — usable for tracking with hardware compression. The 4i4 adds more I/O for recording multiple sources simultaneously. Both are meaningful steps up.
Best
Universal Audio Apollo Twin X or Apollo Solo
~$900-1,200
The Apollo interfaces include Unison preamp technology and access to UA's plugin library running at near-zero latency on dedicated DSP. Industry-standard converters, Neve and API preamp emulations built in. What professional engineers use for serious work. A long-term investment that lasts a career.
🏠
Acoustic Treatment
Only relevant in a fixed space — skip if mobile or renting
Good
GIK Acoustics 242 Panels (starter pack)
~$300-400
GIK makes professional-grade absorption panels at near-DIY prices. A starter pack of 4-6 panels behind and beside your mix position makes a meaningful difference in a small room.
Better
GIK full room treatment package
~$800-1,200
Covering first reflection points, the rear wall, and bass trapping in the corners. This transforms a bedroom or small studio into a genuinely workable mixing environment. GIK's room analysis service tells you exactly what to buy.
Best
Professional room design + custom build
$3,000+
A properly designed room with tuned bass trapping, broadband absorption, and diffusion. Hire an acoustic designer (Acoustic Fields, Ethan Winer) to specify and build. This is where serious mixing studios live.
🎹
MIDI Controller / Performance Surface
For Ableton performance and live sets
Good
Akai MPK Mini or Arturia MiniLab
~$100
25 keys, pads, knobs — everything you need for MIDI production, theory exercises, and Ableton control. Compact enough for camper van use. The starting point.
Better
Novation Launchpad X or Akai APC40 mkII
~$160-200
Purpose-built Ableton controllers. The Launchpad X is a grid of 64 velocity-sensitive pads that maps perfectly to Session View. The APC40 gives you mixer control with faders. Essential for live performance once your set is built.
Best
Ableton Push 3
~$800 (controller) · ~$1,600 (standalone)
Push 3 is Ableton's own instrument — deep integration, 64 pressure-sensitive pads, full mixer control, onboard display. The standalone version runs Ableton without a computer. The most powerful Ableton performance surface made, and the natural end-point for serious Ableton users.
🔊
Headphone Amplifier / DAC
For headphone-only setups — maximizes your MDR-7506 and Focal
Good
FiiO E10K or Behringer UMC202HD
~$80-100
A dedicated headphone amp drives your headphones with more current than a laptop or interface output, improving dynamics and detail. The FiiO E10K is a clean desktop unit. Subtle but real improvement.
Better
Schiit Magni + Modi stack
~$200-250
Schiit's entry-level amp/DAC stack is a significant step up in output quality, noise floor, and headroom. Widely used by engineers who mix primarily on headphones. Compact and very clean.
Best
Dangerous Music D-Box or RME ADI-2 DAC
~$800-1,400
The RME ADI-2 DAC is the reference standard for headphone listening — exceptional converter quality, built-in EQ for headphone correction, ultra-low noise floor. The D-Box adds summing capability. Both are professional tools that fully unlock what a Focal Clear or Listen Professional can do.
